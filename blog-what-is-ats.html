What Is an Applicant Tracking System (And Why It's Rejecting Your Resume)

You applied. You never heard back. You assumed you weren't qualified enough. There's a reasonable chance that's not what happened.

A large percentage of resumes get filtered out before any human opens them. The software doing the filtering is called an applicant tracking system, or ATS. Most college students have never heard of it, which puts them at a structural disadvantage the moment they start applying for internships and jobs.

Here's what it is, how it works, and what you can do about it.

The Basics: What an Applicant Tracking System Does

An applicant tracking system is software that companies use to manage job applications at scale. When you apply through a company's careers page, through LinkedIn, through Indeed, or through any major job board, your application almost certainly flows into an ATS.

The system's job is to do three things. First, it stores all incoming applications in one place so recruiters can manage hundreds or thousands of candidates without losing track of anyone. Second, it parses each resume, meaning it reads your document and extracts structured information: your name, contact details, work history, education, skills, dates. Third, it scores or filters applications based on criteria the recruiter has defined, often by matching parsed text against keywords from the job description.

The result is that by the time a human recruiter opens a queue of resumes to review, many applications have already been screened out. Research on large-company hiring suggests that somewhere around 75% of resumes submitted to major employers are filtered before a human sees them. The exact number varies widely by company and role, but the direction is clear: the system is doing a lot of work before any person gets involved.

Why Your Resume Gets Filtered Out

There are two main reasons a resume gets removed from consideration at the ATS stage: keyword mismatch and parsing failure.

Keyword mismatch is the more intuitive one. If the job description asks for someone with "financial modeling experience" and your resume says "built spreadsheet models in Excel," an ATS doing a strict keyword match may not connect those two things. The recruiter who posted the job may have told the system to prioritize candidates whose resumes contain specific phrases, and if yours doesn't contain them, you score lower or get filtered out entirely.

Parsing failure is less intuitive but equally damaging. Parsing is the process by which the ATS reads your document and attempts to extract your information into structured fields. The problem is that many resume designs break parsers. Columns, tables, text boxes, graphics, headers, footers, and unusual fonts all cause different parsing systems to choke, skip content, or assign information to the wrong field. Your name might end up in the skills field. Your GPA might not be extracted at all. Your contact email might vanish if it lives inside a document header rather than in the body of the page.

When a parser can't correctly read your resume, it doesn't flag it as "broken." It just produces bad data. And bad data often means a low relevance score, which means a human never sees you.

The Companies Behind the Systems

ATS is not a single product. There are dozens of systems on the market, and they all work differently. The major ones you'll encounter as a student applying to internships and entry-level roles include Workday, Greenhouse, Lever, Taleo (owned by Oracle), iCIMS, and SmartRecruiters.

Each of these has its own parsing engine and its own quirks. A resume that parses perfectly in Greenhouse might break in Taleo. A keyword that scores well in Lever might be missed in iCIMS. This matters because when you're applying broadly, you have no way of knowing which system you're applying into.

The only way to protect yourself is to build a resume that parses reliably across systems, not just one.

What Formatting Actually Does to Your ATS Score

The safest resume for ATS purposes is a simple one. Single-column layout. Standard section headers. System fonts. Contact information in the main body of the document, not in a header or footer. No graphics, no skill bar charts, no icons.

This is a hard pill for students who have been told their resume needs to stand out visually. It does need to stand out. But it needs to stand out to a human recruiter who has already seen it pass through the ATS filter. A beautiful resume that parses poorly may never get to that human. A clean, readable resume that parses well will.

The visual impression you make happens after you clear the filter, not before. Keep the design clean and simple, and make your content do the work.

What Keywords Actually Matter

Keywords in ATS context are not mysterious. They are the specific words and phrases from the job description. When a recruiter sets up a job posting, they either manually enter required skills or the system automatically identifies important terms from the posting. Your resume is then scored against those terms.

The practical implication is that every application should involve reading the job description and making sure your resume reflects the language used. If the posting says "Python," your resume should say "Python," not "programming" or "scripting." If it says "project management," use that exact phrase if it accurately describes your experience.

You do not need to use every keyword. Focus on the skills and responsibilities that appear most prominently in the posting and make sure they appear naturally in your resume.

How to Know If Your Resume Is Passing or Failing

The honest answer is that you can't tell just by looking at your own resume. You see a formatted document. The ATS sees raw extracted text, and what it extracts may be very different from what you designed.
