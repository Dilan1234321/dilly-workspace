Smart, Grit, and Build: The Three Things That Actually Define Your Career Readiness

GPA tells you one thing. Dilly tells you three. And those three things — Smart, Grit, and Build — are the actual dimensions that predict whether a college student will get competitive internships, strong full-time offers, and a career that accelerates.

This post breaks down exactly what each score means, what signals go into it, and — most importantly — what you can do to improve each one before your next application cycle.

Smart

Your analytical and intellectual signal strength

🔥
Grit

Your drive, persistence, and initiative outside of class

⚒️
Build

Your track record of creating real, tangible things

Smart: What It Actually Measures

Smart is not just GPA. It's your overall academic and analytical signal — the evidence that you can think rigorously, reason through problems, and work at a high cognitive level.

The Smart score draws from several sources:

  • GPA and academic standing — your overall GPA and whether it's trending up or down
  • Course difficulty — honors, advanced, or graduate-level coursework signals more than a high GPA in easy classes
  • Analytical and quantitative coursework — economics, statistics, computer science, data science, finance all signal stronger analytical horsepower
  • Scholarships and academic awards — external validation that your academic performance is recognized
  • Quality of quantified bullets on your resume — if every bullet has a number, that signals analytical thinking in practice

A student with a 3.2 GPA who took Econometrics, Financial Modeling, and wrote a thesis can score higher on Smart than a student with a 3.8 who took only introductory courses. Context matters.

How to Improve Your Smart Score

  • Add numbers to your resume bullets — every single one where possible
  • List relevant coursework, especially quantitative or technical courses
  • Pursue at least one academic credential that demonstrates rigor (Dean's List, honors thesis, competitive research program)
  • If your GPA is trending upward, note it — "GPA: 3.4 (3.7 last semester)" signals improvement

Grit: What It Actually Measures

Grit is the score most students underestimate. It measures whether you do things beyond what's required — whether you show up consistently, push through difficulty, and take initiative when no one is watching.

Recruiters care deeply about Grit because it predicts whether an employee will self-direct or need constant management. The signals:

  • Leadership tenure — holding a leadership role for 2+ semesters signals follow-through, not just joining
  • Volume of meaningful involvement — a student doing 4 substantial things is different from one who listed 12 things they attended once
  • Evidence of overcoming obstacles — working while in school, transferring and maintaining performance, rebuilding a GPA
  • Self-directed learning — certifications, online courses, workshops you sought out on your own
  • Long-term commitment to anything — the same sport, instrument, volunteer role, or organization over multiple years

The Grit trap: Many students pad their resume with a long list of clubs and organizations. Grit doesn't reward quantity — it rewards depth. Two organizations where you held leadership positions and created something beats twelve organizations where you were a member.

How to Improve Your Grit Score

  • Pick 2-3 organizations and go deep: run for leadership, start initiatives, take ownership
  • Document persistence: if you worked during school, that belongs on your resume
  • Get certifications in things relevant to your field (Google Analytics, Bloomberg Market Concepts, AWS Cloud Practitioner)
  • Remove organizations where you were just a member — they dilute your Grit signal, not strengthen it

Build: What It Actually Measures

Build is the newest and fastest-growing dimension that recruiters evaluate. It measures whether you've created something real — a product, a system, a project, an event, a company — that exists in the world beyond a classroom assignment.

The builder mentality is now the most valued trait in college hiring, particularly in tech, consulting, finance, and entrepreneurship. Build signals include:

  • Projects with tangible deliverables — apps, models, websites, reports, research papers, business plans
  • Entrepreneurial experience — starting anything: a club, a business, a student organization, a side hustle
  • Open-source or public work — GitHub repos, published writing, Kaggle notebooks, design portfolios
  • Cross-functional creation — building something that required combining multiple skills (code + design, finance + communication)
  • Scale and reach — projects that reached real users, clients, or an audience

How to Improve Your Build Score

  • Start one substantial project this semester with a public-facing deliverable
  • Post your work publicly: GitHub, personal website, Behance, Substack
  • Document your build process — case competitions, hackathons, independent research all count
  • If you've freelanced or done independent work, frame it as a business (even micro-scale)

Why All Three Together Matters More Than Any One Score

The most common resume profiles we see at Dilly fall into one of these patterns:

  • High Smart, Low Grit, Low Build — the academic with a 3.9 GPA who has only taken classes and has no real-world output. Gets filtered out by companies looking for self-starters.
  • High Grit, Low Smart, Low Build — the networker who has led every club but can't show analytical work. Gets noticed but can't close technical interviews.
  • High Build, Low Smart, Low Grit — the person who built one impressive thing but has no depth. Gets attention but can't show it wasn't a one-time fluke.

The students who consistently land top internships at Goldman, Google, McKinsey, or top startups have balanced profiles across all three. They're smart enough to do the work, gritty enough to be self-directed, and builders who have a portfolio of real output.
