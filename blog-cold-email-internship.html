How to Cold Email for Internships: Templates That Actually Get Replies

Most students treat cold email like a lottery ticket — send a bunch, expect nothing, be mildly surprised if anything happens. That's the wrong mental model. Cold email is a skill, and like any skill, the difference between people who get replies and people who don't comes down to a handful of concrete things they're doing differently.

This guide is going to be specific. Not "be personalized and professional," but here's the exact structure, here are two templates, here are the specific mistakes that kill your reply rate.

Why Cold Email Works (Especially for Small Companies)

At big companies — the Googles, the Goldman Sachs of the world — cold email to random employees is mostly noise. They have formal recruiting pipelines, HR departments, and applicant tracking systems designed to process volume. Your cold email to a VP there is getting forwarded to a recruiter inbox and forgotten.

But at companies with 20 to 200 employees? Cold email is often the only way in. These companies frequently don't post on job boards. They don't have dedicated campus recruiting programs. A well-written email to the right person at a small company can land you an internship that isn't available anywhere else — and with far less competition than a role that 400 students applied to through a portal.

Even at larger companies, cold email to alumni or employees in specific departments — not HR — works better than the generic application process. You're bypassing the filter and getting to someone who can actually advocate for you.

The Anatomy of a Cold Email That Gets a Reply

A good cold email has four parts. Each one has a job to do.

1. The Subject Line

Your subject line's only job is to get the email opened. It should be specific, short (under 50 characters), and not look like spam. Avoid subject lines that sound like mass outreach: "Internship Inquiry — Summer 2026" is generic. "Finance junior from UT — quick question about your M&A team" is better. It signals you're a real person with a specific ask, not someone blasting the same message to 100 people.

2. The Opener

Your first sentence should establish who you are and why you're emailing this specific person. Not "I came across your profile on LinkedIn" — everyone writes that. Something concrete: you read their blog post, you heard them speak at a conference, you noticed they worked at a company you interned at, or most powerfully, you went to the same school.

3. The Body

Keep it to three to four sentences. Say what you're studying, something specific you've done (one concrete thing — a project, a role, something you built), and why you're interested in their company specifically. Not "I'm passionate about your mission." Something real: you use their product, you find the problem space interesting, you've been following a specific thing they're working on.

4. The Ask

This is where most students blow it. They either ask for a job directly (too big of an ask from a stranger) or they're so vague about what they want that the person doesn't know how to respond. The ideal ask is a 20-minute call to learn about their experience or the team. That's it. You're not asking them to hire you. You're asking for conversation. That's a much smaller yes to say.

Template 1: Cold Email to a Small Company

Template — Small Company / Direct Outreach
To: [First name at company — ideally a founder, team lead, or someone in the function you want]
Subject: Finance junior from UT — quick question about your team
Hi [First name], I'm a junior studying finance at the University of Tampa. I came across [Company] while researching [specific thing — fintech companies working on credit risk, climate startups in the Southeast, whatever is real], and spent some time on your site — the work you're doing on [specific product or initiative] is genuinely interesting to me. I built a small stock-screening tool last year that [brief relevant detail] and I'm looking to apply that hands-on approach somewhere over the summer. I'd love to learn more about how your team works and whether there's a fit for a summer intern. Would you be open to a 20-minute call? Happy to work around your schedule. Thanks, [Your name] [School, graduation year] [Phone / LinkedIn URL]

A few notes on this template: the subject line is personalized without being weird. The body is four short paragraphs that move quickly. The ask is easy to say yes to. The sign-off includes your LinkedIn so they can immediately check you out without effort.

Template 2: Reaching Out to Alumni

Template — Alumni Outreach
To: [Alumnus or alumna at target company — find on LinkedIn]
Subject: Fellow [School] alum — hoping to ask about [Company]
Hi [First name], I found your profile on LinkedIn — I'm a junior at [School] studying [major], and I saw you graduated from the same program in [year]. Congrats on the role at [Company]. I'm exploring opportunities in [field] this summer and [Company] keeps coming up as a place people I respect seem to end up. I'd love to ask you a few questions about what the team culture is like and what the internship experience was like if you had one. If you have 15-20 minutes for a quick call in the next few weeks, I'd really appreciate it. No pressure at all if you're slammed. [Your name] [Graduation year, major] [LinkedIn URL]

The alumni angle is your single most powerful asset as a current student. Alumni reply at dramatically higher rates than random employees. The shared school connection is a genuine social bond — people want to help people from their school. Use it. Don't be shy about it.

Common Mistakes That Kill Your Reply Rate

Too long

If your email requires scrolling on a phone, it's too long. Nobody owes you their attention. Three to five sentences in the body is the ceiling. Every extra sentence you write reduces your reply rate.

Vague ask

"I would love to connect and explore potential opportunities" is not an ask — it's a sentence that means nothing. Say specifically what you want: a 20-minute call, an answer to one question, an introduction to someone else on the team. Concrete asks get concrete responses.

Flattery overload

"I am incredibly inspired by your journey and find your work absolutely fascinating" reads as desperate and hollow. One genuine, specific observation about their work is worth more than three paragraphs of compliments. Specificity is respect. Generic flattery is noise.

Attaching your resume unsolicited

Don't attach your resume to the first cold email. You haven't earned that yet. You're asking for a conversation, not a job application review. Bring it up if they ask, or mention it's available if they'd like it — don't lead with it.

How to Find the Right Person's Email

Start with LinkedIn. If the company is small enough that their employees are visible, you can often find the person you want and then guess their email format. Most company emails follow one of a few patterns: firstname@company.com, first.last@company.com, or firstinitial+last@company.com.

Hunter.io is a free tool that lets you search for email addresses by company domain and often shows you the format the company uses. It's not perfect but it's usually accurate. RocketReach and Apollo are alternatives if Hunter doesn't have what you need.

If you can't find their email, try LinkedIn's direct message feature (InMail). It's less effective than email but better than nothing. You can also sometimes find emails in conference speaker bios, GitHub profiles, or personal websites.

One small trick Before you send, Google the person's name + their company + "email" to see if it shows up anywhere publicly. You'd be surprised how often this works. Also check if the company has a general contact@ or team@ address you can use as a fallback — someone reads those.

Following Up: When and How

If you don't get a reply in five to seven business days, follow up once. Just once. Don't send three follow-ups over two weeks — that crosses into pestering territory and will close the door permanently.

Your follow-up should be short — two sentences. You're not re-pitching yourself. You're just checking that your email didn't get lost. Something like:

"Hi [Name] — wanted to follow up in case my earlier email got buried. Happy to send along my resume if helpful, or just to connect over a quick call. Either way, hope you're having a good week."

Then leave it. If they don't reply to the follow-up, move on. Some people are just bad at email, some people are genuinely busy, some companies aren't hiring — it's rarely personal. The math on cold email is that you need volume to make it work. Send 30 targeted emails, expect 5 to 8 replies, convert 2 to 3 of those into actual calls. That's a good pipeline.

Make Sure Your Resume Is Ready Before You Send

Cold email gets you the conversation. Your resume closes it. Score yours on Smart, Grit, and Build — and run an ATS check — before you start sending.
