What GPA Do You Need for an Internship? The Real Answer by Industry

The GPA question is one of the most anxiety-inducing parts of internship applications. Students either obsess over a number that doesn't matter as much as they think, or they dismiss it entirely when it actually is a hard filter. The honest answer depends entirely on what industry and what companies you're targeting.

The Short Answer

If your GPA is below 3.0, it will be a significant obstacle at many larger firms. Between 3.0 and 3.5, you're in a gray zone that varies by industry. Above 3.5, GPA is rarely the reason you don't get an interview. But there's a lot of nuance in the middle, and the industry you're going into matters enormously.

GPA Requirements by Industry

Corporate Finance / Accounting 3.3+ Soft filter — often reviewed with context
Technology (FAANG / Big Tech) No official cutoff Flexible — skills and projects matter more

Marketing / Advertising None to 3.0 Flexible — portfolio and creativity matter more
Government / Nonprofits 3.0 for most federal programs Soft filter, often explicitly stated in posting
Media / Publishing None Flexible — clips and experience matter more

Why Finance and Consulting Filter on GPA

Finance and consulting firms receive thousands of applications for a small number of seats. They use GPA as an initial screening signal precisely because it's a quick proxy for whether a candidate can handle the workload and conceptual rigor of the job. It doesn't mean they think GPA perfectly predicts performance — they don't. It means they use it because it's fast and correlated enough.

At the analyst level for investment banking, a GPA below 3.5 at a non-target school, or below 3.3 at a target school, will often be filtered out before a human ever sees the resume. The ATS does it automatically.

At consulting firms, the bar is similar. McKinsey, Bain, and BCG unofficially target 3.5+, and their campus recruiting processes reflect that. Regional consulting firms and Big 4 advisory practices tend to be more flexible, with 3.3 being a more realistic floor.

Why Tech Is Different

Technology companies, especially in software engineering and product roles, have largely moved away from GPA as a primary screen. This happened partly because some of the best engineers in the industry came from non-traditional paths, and partly because coding interviews and take-home projects can assess technical skills directly.

That said, some large tech companies do have unofficial GPA preferences for new grad and intern programs. The practical reality is that a 3.2 from a strong CS program with a solid GitHub portfolio will get a callback at most tech companies where a 3.2 applying to Goldman might not.

The target school exception At investment banks and consulting firms that do target school recruiting, a lower GPA from a target school sometimes receives different treatment than the same GPA from a non-target. Conversely, a 3.8 from a non-target still faces structural barriers at elite firms regardless of GPA. The brand of the institution matters independently.

What to Do If Your GPA Is Below the Cutoff

If your GPA puts you below the threshold for firms you're targeting, you have a few options — and pretending it doesn't matter isn't one of them.

Don't list it — carefully

If your GPA is below 3.0, or below a firm's stated threshold, leaving it off your resume is a common and accepted practice. This is not deception — it's an omission. Recruiters know that students without GPAs on their resumes often have lower ones. It gets you past the ATS filter sometimes, but you'll likely be asked in an interview. Have an honest answer ready about what happened and what you've done since.

Calculate a higher-GPA alternative

If your major GPA, your last-two-years GPA, or your upper-division GPA is notably higher than your cumulative GPA, you can list that instead. "Major GPA: 3.6" is accurate and more favorable if your overall is dragged down by early courses. Just label it correctly.

Make the rest of the resume work harder

GPA is one signal among many. If yours is weak, the rest of your application needs to be proportionally stronger: more specific project descriptions, more quantified accomplishments, stronger leadership evidence. A 3.1 with a compelling project track record is more interesting than a 3.1 with generic job duty bullets.

Target different parts of the market

The firms with hard GPA cutoffs are a subset of the internship market, not all of it. Tech, startups, media, marketing, government, and many mid-size firms in every industry do not hard-screen on GPA. A strategic redirect to those markets is more productive than battering against a hard filter.

What Actually Matters More Than GPA

For the majority of internship roles, what gets you an interview is the combination of your skills, your demonstrated experience, and how well your resume communicates both. GPA matters for a specific slice of the market — important to know, but not the whole picture.

The students who struggle to get internship interviews most often have resumes with weak or absent projects, generic experience descriptions with no numbers or outcomes, and little evidence of initiative outside of class. Those problems are fixable regardless of GPA.
