The Story

Built by a Student
Who Was Tired of Losing.

Not losing at life — losing to a system that was never designed for people like us. A system that filters out great candidates before a single human being reads their name.

It Started With a Rejection Email.

I'm Dilan. I'm 20 years old, studying Data Science at the University of Tampa, and I built Dilly because I watched a friend — one of the smartest, most prepared people I know — get auto-rejected by three companies in the same week.

He never got a reason. He never got feedback. He just got three identical "we've decided to move forward with other candidates" emails, back to back, before anyone had ever looked at his resume.

I knew something was wrong. Not with him. With the system.

The System Is Rigged Against You.

Here's what nobody tells you freshman year: most companies don't read your resume first. A piece of software does. Applicant Tracking Systems — ATS — scan your resume for keywords, formatting, and structure before it ever reaches a recruiter. If your resume doesn't pass, it's gone. No callback. No explanation.

75% of resumes are rejected this way. Three out of four. And the students who don't know this are applying over and over into silence, thinking they're not good enough — when the real problem is a checkbox they never knew existed.

"I kept applying. I kept getting ignored. I thought I just wasn't ready. It turned out my resume had a table in it — and that one thing was breaking every ATS that touched it." — University of Tampa student, Junior year

When I learned how this worked, I got angry. Not at companies — they're dealing with hundreds of applicants. I got angry that nobody was telling us. That the information existed, that tools existed, but they were either too expensive, too generic, or built for mid-career professionals who already had three jobs on their resume.

Nobody was building this for us. So I did.

What I Wanted to Build.

I didn't want to build another resume template generator. I didn't want to build a job board. I wanted to build the thing I wish I'd had — something that told me, honestly and specifically, where I stood and what to do about it.

Not a generic score. Not "add more keywords." I wanted a tool that compared me to the actual students competing for the same roles I wanted. That scanned my resume against the real ATS systems those companies use. That rewrote my bullet points the way a career advisor who actually knew my field would — not a bot regurgitating advice from 2018.

I wanted something that made me feel like I had a coach. Not a $200/hour coach that most of us can't afford. A coach that was always there, always updated, and cost less than a DoorDash order.

That's Dilly.

The Part That Keeps Me Going.

A few months after I launched Dilly, a student I'd never met sent me a message. She said she'd been applying for six months with no response. She ran her resume through Dilly, found out it was failing three different ATS systems for formatting reasons she'd never heard of, fixed them, and got two callbacks the following week.

She said: "I thought I just wasn't good enough."

She was always good enough. She just didn't have the right information.

That's what this is about. Not giving anyone an unfair advantage — giving everyone the information that some people already have. The students with connected parents, alumni networks, expensive coaches. We deserve to know what they know. And now we do.
