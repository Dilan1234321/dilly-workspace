College Resume Template: What to Include (With Examples)

Most college resume templates are designed for professionals with 10 years of experience. That's not helpful when you're a sophomore applying to your first internship and wondering what to put where. This guide is a resume template built specifically for students — the right sections, in the right order, with examples you can actually use.

The Right Sections for a College Resume

The sections on your resume should tell a coherent story about who you are as a student and early professional. For most undergraduates, that story is built from five to six sections. Here they are in the order that works best for internship applications.

  1. Contact information
  2. Education
  3. Experience (internships, part-time jobs, research, relevant campus roles)
  4. Projects (class projects, personal projects, anything you built)
  5. Skills
  6. Leadership and Activities

That order isn't mandatory. If your most impressive section is a research position, put Experience second. If you have no work experience at all, lean harder on Projects and Activities. The rule is: your strongest material goes near the top.

What to Write in Each Section

Contact Information

Name, professional email address (first.last@school.edu is ideal), phone number, and LinkedIn. Add GitHub, a portfolio link, or your personal site if it's relevant to the field you're applying to. Don't add your home address — it wastes space and provides no value to a recruiter.

Education

School name, your degree and major, expected graduation date, and GPA if it's 3.3 or above. Add relevant coursework if you're light on experience — it signals you've been building skills even without work experience. Honors, Dean's List, and merit scholarships belong here too.

Experience

Every bullet should describe an action and its result. The single most common mistake: describing what the job was instead of what you accomplished in it. "Responsible for social media" tells a recruiter nothing. "Grew Instagram engagement by 34% over 10 weeks by shifting posting schedule based on analytics data" tells them a lot.

Use numbers wherever you can. Not because they're impressive, but because they're specific. "Tutored 5 students" is more credible than "tutored students." "Managed $4,000 event budget" is more interesting than "managed budget."

Tip: The three-part bullet A good bullet has three parts: what you did (action), how you did it (method), and what happened (result). "Redesigned the club newsletter [action] using Canva and reader feedback [method], increasing click-through rate from 12% to 29% [result]." Not every bullet needs all three, but the more you can include, the stronger they are.

Projects

This section is where many students undersell themselves. Class projects count — especially if they involved real data, real tools, or a real deliverable. Personal projects count even more. If you've built an app, written a research paper, launched a side business, or designed a system for a club, that belongs here. Add the tools and technologies used, and describe what the project actually was and what it produced.

Skills

Keep this specific. "Microsoft Office" is expected and adds no value. "Advanced Excel (pivot tables, VLOOKUP, Power Query)" is better. List tools and technologies relevant to the roles you're applying for, plus any certifications or credentials that apply to your field. Language fluency is worth listing if you have it.

Leadership and Activities

This is your Grit section — the evidence that you take on responsibility, lead things, and follow through. Club officer roles, athletic commitments, volunteer positions sustained over time. Don't just list the club name; describe what you did in it. "Finance Club member" tells a recruiter nothing. "Finance Club — VP of Operations; organized 6 speaker events per semester and managed $12,000 annual budget" tells them a lot.

The Most Common Formatting Mistakes

Too long. A one-page resume is almost always correct for undergraduates. The only exception: you genuinely have substantial research output or two or more strong internships. If you're padding to reach two pages, cut it.

Inconsistent formatting. All dates should be in the same format (Jun 2025 or June 2025, not both). All bullet points should end the same way (with periods or without, not both). Margins, font sizes, and section headers should be consistent throughout.

Generic objective statement. Remove it. Recruiters do not find value in "Seeking a challenging internship where I can leverage my skills." If you want a header statement, make it a two-line professional summary that is specific to the role you're applying for.

Wrong file format. Send a PDF unless the application explicitly asks for a .docx. PDF preserves your formatting. Word documents sometimes don't.

How to Know if Your Resume Is Actually Working

The template is a starting point. Whether it's working depends on the specific roles you're targeting, the strength of your bullets, and how your profile compares to other applicants on the same tracks.
