The Best Free Resume Tools for College Students (Honest Review)

Every student needs a resume, and almost every student builds their first one using whatever free tool they find first. Most of those tools are fine for getting a document together. Very few of them tell you anything about whether that document will actually perform well in a job application.

This is an honest look at five options: Google Docs, LinkedIn's resume builder, Canva, Zety, and Dilly. What each one does well, where each one falls short, and which situations each one is suited for.

Google Docs Resume Templates

Google Docs is probably the most-used resume tool among college students, and it makes sense. It's free, accessible anywhere, easy to share, and the built-in templates are clean enough to work with. The "Coral," "Modern Writer," and "Serif" templates are genuinely usable starting points.

The biggest advantage of Google Docs is format stability. The single-column templates produce documents that parse well in most applicant tracking systems, which is more than you can say for tools that push multi-column or graphic-heavy designs. You can also export directly to DOCX or PDF, giving you flexibility in what you submit.

The limitation is that Google Docs gives you no feedback. It's a blank canvas. You can write anything, structure it any way, and the tool has no opinion about whether what you've written is effective. There's no scoring, no benchmarking, no ATS test, and no guidance on whether your content is competitive for the roles you're targeting. It's a word processor, not a coaching tool.

Best for: Students who know what they're doing and just need a clean, portable document.

LinkedIn Resume Builder

LinkedIn's resume builder exports your LinkedIn profile into a formatted resume document. If your LinkedIn is detailed and well-maintained, this can produce a reasonable first draft with minimal effort. For students who keep their LinkedIn updated as they go, it removes the friction of maintaining two separate versions of the same information.

The output quality varies a lot depending on what's in your profile. If your LinkedIn is thin, your resume will be thin. The templates LinkedIn produces are functional but visually generic, and the formatting tends toward layouts that include graphics and multi-column sections, which can cause issues with ATS parsers.

The deeper problem is that LinkedIn's resume output is built for LinkedIn's ecosystem. It's oriented around making your profile look full, not around making a document that performs well when submitted to a company's careers page. The keyword optimization suggestions it offers are based on LinkedIn job matching, not on how the specific ATS system a company uses will score your document.

Best for: Getting a quick first draft if your LinkedIn is already detailed.

Canva

Canva makes beautiful resume templates. The visual design quality is genuinely high, and students are drawn to it because the results look polished and professional immediately.

The problem is that beautiful in Canva often means dangerous for ATS. Most Canva resume templates use multi-column layouts, decorative elements, text boxes, custom fonts, icons, and graphic skill bars. These features look good on screen and as a printed document. They parse terribly in most ATS systems. Text in the wrong column gets merged, skills in icon-based charts become invisible to parsers, and contact information in decorative headers often doesn't extract correctly.

Canva resumes are well-suited for specific situations: applying directly to small companies that review resumes manually, submitting a portfolio piece, or any application channel where a human sees the document first. For applications submitted through major job boards or corporate careers portals, a Canva resume is a risk that often isn't worth taking.

Canva does offer some single-column, simpler templates that parse better, but you have to seek them out deliberately among the more visually elaborate options. There's also no feedback mechanism. Like Google Docs, it's a design tool with no opinion on your content.

Best for: Creative fields where design is part of the job and applications go directly to a person, not an ATS.

Zety

Zety is a dedicated resume builder with a guided workflow. It walks you through each section, offers writing suggestions and phrasing prompts, and produces a formatted document at the end. For students who feel stuck staring at a blank page, the guided approach is genuinely helpful. The phrasing suggestions can push you toward more effective language than you might write on your own.

The catch is the free tier. Zety lets you build a resume through the guided workflow for free, but it requires a paid subscription to download the finished document. Some students don't realize this until they've filled out the entire form. The paid plans are reasonable in cost ($3 to $6 per month depending on the plan), but for a student who just wants to export one document, it's an unexpected gate.

The templates Zety uses have improved over time and most of them are ATS-safe at the standard tier. The writing guidance is probably the strongest feature, but it's fairly generic. It doesn't know whether you're applying to a data science internship or a marketing role, and the suggestions don't adapt meaningfully to your specific target.

Best for: Students who want guided writing help and are okay with either the free limitations or paying for the download.

Dilly

Dilly is different from the other tools on this list in one important way: it's not just helping you build a resume. It's telling you how competitive that resume is for your specific career track.

When you upload your resume to Dilly, it scores you on three dimensions: Smart (academics and intellectual credentials), Grit (leadership, persistence, responsibility), and Build (projects, experience, and tangible output). Those scores are then benchmarked against other verified college students on the same career track. If you're a finance major targeting investment banking, you're being compared to other finance students going for the same roles, not a generic population.

Dilly also runs your resume through six ATS parser systems and shows you the output of each one. You can see which fields were extracted correctly, where content was scrambled, and which formatting choices are causing problems in specific systems. That level of visibility doesn't exist in any other free tool.

The free tier covers the full score, ATS scan, and cohort benchmarking with a verified .edu email. The Pro plan at $9.99 per month adds the AI coach feature, which knows your profile and can answer specific questions about what to improve, how to approach a gap in your record, and what internships you should be targeting based on where you stand relative to your peers.

The honest limitation is that Dilly is most useful once you have a resume to work with. It doesn't help you build one from scratch. The expected workflow is: build your first draft in Google Docs or a similar tool, then upload it to Dilly to understand how it performs and where you stand competitively.

Best for: Students who want to know if their resume is actually competitive, not just whether it looks good.

The Bottom Line

If you're building a resume from scratch, start with Google Docs. Use one of the single-column templates, keep it clean, and focus on writing strong, specific content. If you want guided help with phrasing, add a pass through Zety before you finalize.

Once you have a draft, the question that none of these tools answer except Dilly is: am I actually competitive? You can have a well-formatted resume with good content and still be behind the median for students on your career track without knowing it. That information changes what you prioritize, whether that's filling in a project gap, improving your GPA narrative, or getting a leadership role in a club before next recruiting season.

The tools that help you format are useful. The tool that tells you where you actually stand is different.
