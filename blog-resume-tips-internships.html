Resume Tips for College Internships That Actually Work in 2026

There's a lot of generic resume advice out there. Use action verbs. Quantify your impact. Keep it to one page. Most of it is not wrong, but it doesn't tell you why your resume isn't getting responses when you're doing all those things.

The bigger issue is usually structural. The way most students build their first internship resume reflects a fundamental misunderstanding of what recruiters and hiring managers are looking for when they read a student's application. This guide is about fixing that.

The Mistakes That Show Up on Almost Every Student Resume

The most common one is listing job duties instead of accomplishments. "Assisted with social media content" tells a recruiter nothing about your ability. "Wrote 12 Instagram posts over 8 weeks that averaged 40% higher engagement than the account's baseline" tells them everything they need to know. The difference isn't embellishment. It's specificity.

Numbers do not need to be dramatic. They just need to be real and concrete. "Tutored 6 students in calculus, 4 of whom improved their grade by at least one letter" is compelling. "Helped students with math" is forgettable.

The second mistake is resume length confusion. A sophomore applying to their first internship does not need two pages. Recruiters at large companies can spend 7 to 10 seconds on an initial screen. A single clean, dense page is almost always better. The exception is if you genuinely have substantial research, publication, or project output, in which case two pages is fine. If you're padding to fill two pages with repeated responsibility descriptions, cut it to one.

The third mistake is the objective statement. A generic sentence at the top like "Seeking a challenging internship opportunity where I can apply my skills and grow professionally" adds zero information. Either remove it entirely or replace it with a two-line professional summary that is specific to the role you're applying for: your relevant background, your strongest skill, and what you bring to that particular team.

The fourth mistake is not tailoring. Recruiters can tell when you've sent the same resume to every company. Matching your language to the job description is not gaming the system. It's communication. If a company says they need someone who can "synthesize data from multiple sources," and you've done exactly that, use that language.

What Recruiters Are Actually Looking For in an Intern

When a recruiter reviews a college student's resume, they are not expecting a polished professional with years of experience. They are looking for signal. They want evidence that you can learn, that you take initiative, and that you have started building relevant skills even without full-time work experience.

Those three dimensions map pretty cleanly to three questions. What do your academics say about your intellectual horsepower? What have you done that shows you push through difficulty and take on responsibility? What have you actually built, shipped, researched, or contributed to?

These aren't arbitrary categories. They're the signal that separates students who get interview callbacks from those who don't.

Think in Three Dimensions: Smart, Grit, Build

Dilly scores resumes on three dimensions, and they're worth understanding as a framework for what to put on your resume, not just as a scoring tool.

Smart covers your academic record: GPA, relevant coursework, academic awards, scholarships, Dean's List. If your GPA is above a 3.3, include it. If you took advanced coursework or did research for credit, put that on there. Internship recruiters, especially at larger companies, do screen on academics as a proxy for learning ability.

Grit covers leadership, persistence, and responsibility. Club officer roles, athletic commitments, jobs held during school, volunteer work sustained over time, projects you started and saw through to completion. This is where students undersell themselves. If you held a part-time job while carrying a full course load, that's signal. If you led your case competition team through three rounds over a semester, that belongs prominently on your resume.

Build covers what you've made, built, or contributed to. Class projects that resulted in something tangible, personal projects in your field, research presentations, apps, case studies, published writing, events you organized. Recruiters in most fields respond strongly to students who have built things, even if those things are small in scope.

When you look at your resume through this lens, weak sections become obvious. If you have almost nothing under Grit, your resume has no narrative about who you are as a person beyond your grades. If Build is empty, you look like someone who has only been in class, never doing anything with what they've learned.

How to Structure Your Resume for an Internship Application

The order of sections matters and should reflect what's most impressive about you at this stage. For most college students applying to internships, this works well:

  1. Contact information (name, phone, email, LinkedIn, GitHub or portfolio if relevant)
  2. Education (school, major, GPA if 3.3 or above, graduation date, relevant coursework)
  3. Experience (internships, part-time jobs, research positions, relevant on-campus roles)
  4. Projects (class projects, personal projects, anything you built or contributed to)
  5. Skills (tools, languages, certifications, relevant technical or domain skills)
  6. Leadership and Activities (clubs, athletics, community service, anything sustained and meaningful)

If you have a strong internship or research experience, move Experience above Education. If your projects are your strongest asset, move Projects above Experience. The rule is: your most impressive material should appear first.

The Question Your Resume Needs to Answer

Before you submit, read through your resume and ask yourself: does this document make it obvious why someone should pick me over another student from a similar school with similar grades? If the answer is no, the problem is almost always insufficient specificity or a missing dimension.

Adding one concrete project, one leadership role with actual scope, or one quantified accomplishment can shift a resume from forgettable to competitive. You probably already have the material. The work is in how you present it.
