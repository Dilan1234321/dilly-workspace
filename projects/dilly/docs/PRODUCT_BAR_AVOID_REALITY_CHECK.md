# Product Bar: So Good We Don't Hit the Reality-Check Problems

**Purpose:** For each risk in REALITY_CHECK.md, define what "that good" means and what we build to get there. Product as the lever to avoid the holes.

---

## 1. Will students actually pay? → First experience has to be *unmissable*

**Bar:** A user who completes the first free audit thinks: "I cannot apply anywhere without fixing this. $10 is nothing compared to blowing an interview."

**What we build / improve:**

- **First audit is undeniably better than free options.** Evidence is cited from *their* resume. Recommendations are copy-paste ready. Track-specific so it's obvious ChatGPT and the career center can't do this. No generic fluff.
- **Paywall moment is value, not friction.** Right after first audit: show them exactly what they'd get next (re-audit to see progress, job-fit for the role they want, ATS for the company they're targeting). The "what you're missing" is concrete, not vague.
- **Proof in the product.** "4 PA interviews after Meridian" (or equivalent) visible where it matters — not buried. Outcome stories tied to the track they're in. So the decision is "this works for people like me."
- **Track-specific from second one.** Onboarding and first run feel native to Pre-Health / Tech / etc. So they don't think "this is generic, I could use ChatGPT."

**Metric we're chasing:** Conversion from first-audit completer → paid. If that's strong, we're not hitting the "won't pay" problem.

---

## 2. Distribution is brutal → Product has to *spread* and *sell itself*

**Bar:** Users want to share it. One user brings others. The product gives a reason to share and a reason for schools to say "we want that."

**What we build / improve:**

- **Shareable outcomes that don't feel like ads.** Share card / snapshot: "Top 15% Grit, Pre-Health" or "Smart 78, Build 72 — Tech." One-tap share to LinkedIn, Handshake note, career fair follow-up. So good they *want* to show it. Link back to Meridian.
- **Referral that rewards both sides.** Invite a friend → both get something real (e.g. free month). The invite flow is in-product and easy. So growth is built in, not only paid.
- **Cohort report that sells to career centers.** Batch audit + report: "Pre-Health cohort average, distribution, who's on track." So when we walk into a career center we're not just "another tool" — we're "here's what your students get, here's the report you get." Product does the demo.
- **School-specific proof.** One school (e.g. UTampa) with clear before/after or outcome stats. Product + positioning make that story easy to tell and believe.

**Metric we're chasing:** Invites/referrals per user, and "career center can see value in one report." So we're not relying only on cold outbound.

---

## 3. Retention and churn → Product has to be *used every week*

**Bar:** Meridian is part of their application workflow. They open it for every role they care about, and at least weekly during recruiting season. Not "audit once and leave."

**What we build / improve:**

- **Job-fit is central.** "Am I Ready?" for company/role is so useful they run it before every application. So Meridian is the gate before they apply — habit, not one-off.
- **ATS by company.** "Amazon uses Workday — here's your score and what to fix." Tied to *their* target list. So they come back for every target company.
- **Deadlines and "one thing to do."** Calendar + next action so there's always a reason to open the app. Not just "your scores" but "your Goldman deadline is in 8 days; here's the one thing that will move the needle."
- **Voice that solves real questions.** "How do I explain this gap?" "What should I say in the interview for this role?" Answers grounded in *their* resume and *their* track. So Voice is the reason they don't go to ChatGPT.
- **Progress that feels real.** "Last time vs this time" + "why your scores changed." So they see improvement and want to keep improving. Re-audit is a feature, not an afterthought.

**Metric we're chasing:** Month-2 and month-3 retention. If we're strong there, we're not hitting the "one-and-done" problem.

---

## 4. Credit score / signal → Product has to make outcomes *obvious and credible*

**Bar:** We don't depend on recruiters adopting the score yet. We depend on "I used Meridian and got more interviews" being true and visible. So the product creates the outcome; the story sells.

**What we build / improve:**

- **Outcome capture (lightweight).** "Did you get an interview / offer?" optional prompt after they've been using Meridian for a while. So we can measure and say "X% of active users got an interview" (with permission). Product generates the proof.
- **Shareable score that looks like a credential.** Six-second profile / share card is professional enough to put in a Handshake note or LinkedIn. So the score is *used* in the application process even if recruiters don't "verify" it yet.
- **Track-specific so the story is specific.** "Pre-Health, 4 PA interviews" not "got a job." So the outcome is credible for the segment we're selling to.

**Metric we're chasing:** % of active users who report interview/offer (and permission to use it). So we're not betting on recruiter adoption to have a story.

## 5. Competition and "good enough" → Product has to be *obviously different*

**Bar:** A student who tries ChatGPT or Quinncia and then Meridian thinks: "This is not the same. This knows my track and my resume. The others don't."

**What we build / improve:**

- **Track is visible everywhere.** Playbooks, scores, recommendations, and Voice all reference *their* track (Pre-Health, Tech, etc.). No generic mode. So "track-specific" isn't a bullet point — it's the experience.
- **Evidence and "cited from your resume" everywhere.** Every recommendation ties to a specific line or section. So it's obvious we're not generic. ChatGPT can't do that; Quinncia doesn't do it like we do.
- **Copy-paste fixes, not just advice.** Line edits with one-tap copy. So the gap between "what to do" and "done" is zero. That's a tangible differentiator.
- **Company- and role-specific.** "Am I Ready for Goldman?" "What does Amazon's ATS see?" So we're not "resume tips" — we're "your readiness for *this* role at *this* company."
- **Ship and talk about what others don't have.** Per-ATS vendor simulation, company→ATS lookup, job-fit, explain-delta. Document and pitch it so the gap is obvious.

**Metric we're chasing:** "Would you recommend Meridian over free alternatives?" and NPS. If the answer is clearly yes, "good enough" doesn't beat us.

---

## 6. Unit economics → Product has to drive LTV and lower CAC

**Bar:** Retention is high enough that LTV supports the business. And the product creates enough organic/viral behavior that we don't depend only on paid acquisition.

**What we build / improve:**

- **Everything in section 3 (retention)** directly raises LTV. So product quality = LTV.
- **Everything in section 2 (distribution)** — shareable outcomes, referral, cohort report — lowers effective CAC. So product = distribution.
- **Pricing and packaging that match value.** If we add deep-dive reports or workshops, we have expansion revenue. So LTV can grow without only raising price.
- **No dark patterns.** We want retention because the product is worth it, not because cancellation is hidden. So when they stay, the unit economics are real.

**Metric we're chasing:** LTV:CAC ratio and % of signups that come from referral or organic. So the business works without infinite ad spend.

---

## Summary: The bar in one sentence

**We don't hit the reality-check problems when:**  
The first audit is so good they feel they can't apply without it; the product is so useful they use it every week and want to share it; outcomes are real and measurable; and the experience is obviously different from free and incumbent tools — so conversion, retention, and word-of-mouth all work.
