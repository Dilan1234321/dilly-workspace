# Meridian — Reality Check

**Purpose:** Poke holes. What can go wrong? Does this have potential? Honest answers.

---

## 1. Will students actually pay?

**The hole:** Career centers are free. Quinncia is often free (school pays). Handshake is free. ChatGPT will "improve my resume" for free. You're asking $9.99/mo for something that has a free (worse) alternative everywhere.

- Many students will never convert. "I'll just go to the career center" or "I'll use ChatGPT" is the default. Your conversion from visitor → paid has to be strong or the unit economics collapse.
- The ones most likely to pay (Pre-Health, Pre-Law, Tech, serious about internships) are a subset of undergrads. TAM is smaller than "all college students."
- If paywall is after first audit, you need that first audit to be so good they *have* to stay. If it's "meh," they churn. One shot.

**Truth:** It has potential *if* you prove that a meaningful % of students in your target tracks will pay. That's not proven yet. You need conversion and retention data, not hope.

---

## 2. Distribution is brutal

**The hole:** You're .edu only. Every school is a new cold start. There's no viral "invite anyone" — you're stuck with campus-by-campus or career-center sales.

- **Campus-by-campus:** Slow. You need champions, proof, word of mouth. One school at a time. Can you survive 2–3 years of that?
- **Career center sales:** Procurement, committees, "we already have Quinncia/Handshake." Sales cycles can be 6–12 months. Do you have the runway and the person to do that?
- **Quinncia has 100+ institutions.** They have distribution. You have better product (on resume/readiness). Better product with no distribution often loses to worse product with distribution.

**Truth:** Distribution is the real business. Product is necessary but not sufficient. If you can't crack "how do we get in front of thousands of students or dozens of schools," the idea stalls.

---

## 3. Retention and churn

**The hole:** Resume audit is somewhat one-and-done. Run it, fix, run again. After 2–3 audits, what's the reason to keep paying?

- You've built Voice, job-fit, ATS, playbooks — that's the stickiness. But does the *average* user use them enough? If most people "audit once and leave," LTV is low and CAC burns you.
- **Students graduate.** Your user base churns every 4 years. You need constant acquisition. That's different from a B2B product where one customer stays 5 years.

**Truth:** You need retention metrics. If month-3 retention is terrible, the model doesn't work no matter how good the product is. If it's strong, you have a business.

---

## 4. "Credit Score for Talent" is a long bet

**The hole:** The vision is "recruiters care about Meridian score." That requires:
- Enough students with scores that recruiters see them.
- Recruiters actually looking at / trusting the score.
- Two-sided adoption. Right now it's one-sided (students). Until recruiters or employers value it, you're a self-improvement tool, not a market signal.

- If you stay "self-improvement tool," you're competing with free (career center, ChatGPT, YouTube). You win on quality and specificity (track-specific, evidence-based). That can work but it's a harder sell.
- If recruiters never care, the "credit score" narrative is marketing, not moat.

**Truth:** The idea has *more* potential if the score becomes a signal. But that's a multi-year bet. Don't bank the business on it. Bank on "students pay because they get better outcomes (interviews, offers)" first.

---

## 5. Competition and "good enough"

**The hole:**
- **Quinncia** has reach. They can improve their product. If they add "track-specific" or "copy-paste edits," your differentiator shrinks.
- **Handshake** could add or buy "readiness." They have the school relationship. They move slow, but they're there.
- **ChatGPT / free tools:** For a lot of students, "improve my resume" in ChatGPT is good enough. They don't know or care about track-specific. You're selling to the segment that cares — that segment is real but not everyone.

**Truth:** You're not doomed. You have a better resume/readiness engine and a clear wedge (track-specific, evidence, job-fit). But competition doesn't stand still. Speed and proof matter.

---

## 6. Unit economics and runway

**The hole:**
- $9.99/mo. If CAC is high (ads, sales) and LTV is 6–12 months (churn), margin is thin. You need either low CAC (organic, viral, career center distribution) or high LTV (retention, expansion).
- If you're solo or tiny team: you're doing product, growth, support, and (if B2B) sales. That's a lot. Burnout and focus are risks.
- Runway: can you survive long enough to prove conversion, retention, and distribution? If not, the idea dies before it gets a real test.

**Truth:** The idea doesn't have to be capital-intensive. But it does need a path to profitability that fits your runway. That path is either: (a) students pay and stay, or (b) career centers/schools pay and give you distribution. One of those has to work.

---

## 7. What has to go right for this to work

- **Students in your target tracks (Pre-Health, Tech, etc.) pay and stay** — proven with conversion + retention data.
- **Or:** Career centers/schools pay (pilot → license) and give you reach — proven with 1–2 signed schools.
- **Outcomes are real** — "I used Meridian and got more interviews" is the story. If that's not true, the product doesn't deserve to win.
- **You don't run out of runway** before you prove one of the above.

---

## 8. Does it have potential?

**Yes.**
- Real problem: resume quality and readiness matter. Recruiters skim; students get one shot. That's true.
- Motivated segment: Pre-Health, Pre-Law, Tech, consulting-bound students care. They'll pay for edge if the product delivers.
- You have a better product on resume/readiness (track-specific, evidence, job-fit, ATS) than the free or incumbent options in that slice. That's a real wedge.
- If you dominate one campus and show outcomes, you have a repeatable playbook. If one career center pays, you have a B2B path.

**But.**
- Potential isn't the same as inevitability. Students might not pay in enough numbers. Distribution might never crack. Competition might copy you or out-distribute you. Runway might run out.
- The idea is not "obviously stupid." It's a real bet. The bet is: "Enough students (or schools) will pay for track-specific resume readiness and we can reach them." That's testable. Test it.

---

## 9. What to do with this

1. **Prove conversion and retention** at one school. Get numbers. If they're bad, fix product or positioning before scaling.
2. **Prove one B2B path** — one career center pilot, one cohort report, one "we'd pay" conversation. If it's impossible, don't bet the company on it.
3. **Don't confuse "great product" with "great business."** Business = product + distribution + unit economics. You're building the product. The other two need the same honesty.
4. **Run the numbers.** What's your CAC? What's LTV if retention is 6 months? 12? What's the minimum you need to be profitable? If the numbers don't work, the idea needs to change (pricing, segment, or channel).

---

**Bottom line:** The idea has potential. It's not a fantasy. But it has real holes: willingness to pay, distribution, retention, and competition. The way through is to test the bet with evidence — conversion, retention, one signed school or one cohort outcome — and be willing to change or quit if the evidence says so. That's the truth.
