# Pre-Health (Medical School) Rubric — Industry Standards

**Purpose:** This rubric is derived from AAMC (Association of American Medical Colleges) frameworks and from published admissions criteria of multiple U.S. medical schools. Use it to score Pre-Health resumes on the same factors that medical school admissions committees use. **Do not invent criteria; only apply what is stated here and what is evidenced in the resume.**

**Sources:** AAMC Premed Competencies (2023/24), AAMC Holistic Review, University of Arizona College of Medicine–Phoenix, University of Pittsburgh School of Medicine, University of Nebraska Medical Center (UNMC), Cooper Medical School of Rowan University (CMSRU), Oakland University William Beaumont School of Medicine (OUWB), and aggregated criteria from Harvard, Stanford, UCSF, Ohio State, Temple, UCI, and similar institutions.

---

## 1. AAMC Premed Competencies for Entering Medical Students

Medical schools evaluate applicants against these competencies. They are organized into three categories. **Show, don’t tell:** evidence comes from experiences, activities, and accomplishments described on the resume, not from claims alone.

### Professional Competencies (10)

| Competency | What to look for on resume / in activities |
|------------|--------------------------------------------|
| **Teamwork and Collaboration** | Shared goals, balancing team member and leader roles, cooperation. |
| **Service Orientation** | Initiative to help others, meaningful contribution to community well-being. |
| **Resilience and Adaptability** | Effectiveness under stress, constructive adjustment to change or setbacks. |
| **Reliability and Dependability** | Following through on commitments, meeting obligations, taking responsibility. |
| **Oral Communication** | Clear speaking, active listening, adjusting message for understanding. |
| **Interpersonal Skills** | Positive relationships, navigating social cues, managing emotions in collaboration. |
| **Ethical Responsibility to Self and Others** | Integrity, honesty, accountability in professional interactions. |
| **Empathy and Compassion** | Understanding others’ experiences, responding with care and support. |
| **Cultural Humility** | Engaging with differing viewpoints, reflecting on biases, promoting inclusivity. |
| **Cultural Awareness** | Recognizing how historical, social, political, economic factors shape behavior; respecting diverse perspectives. |
| **Commitment to Learning and Growth** | Seeking feedback, setting goals, improving through self-assessment. |

### Science Competencies (2)

| Competency | What to look for |
|------------|------------------|
| **Living Systems** | Understanding of biological systems (molecules to organs), health processes, scientific problem-solving. |
| **Human Behavior** | Applying psychological, social, cultural, and biological factors to understand behavior and health outcomes. |

### Thinking and Reasoning Competencies (3)

| Competency | What to look for |
|------------|------------------|
| **Written Communication** | Conveying complex ideas and research clearly in writing. |
| **Scientific Inquiry** | Designing questions, collecting/analyzing data, drawing evidence-based conclusions. |
| **Quantitative Reasoning** | Using math, statistics, and data analysis to interpret information and solve problems. |
| **Critical Thinking** | Evaluating evidence, questioning assumptions, comparing solutions for informed decisions. |

## 2. AAMC Holistic Review — Experiences, Attributes, Academic Metrics (E-A-M)

Holistic review means **balanced consideration** of:

- **Experiences:** What the applicant has done (clinical, research, leadership, community service, extracurriculars).
- **Attributes:** Who the applicant is (integrity, compassion, resilience, cultural competence, service orientation, etc.).
- **Academic metrics:** GPA, BCPM (science GPA), MCAT (when available), course rigor and trends.

**Principles:**

- Nearly all U.S. medical schools use some form of holistic review.
- Criteria are **mission-based**: schools weight factors differently based on institutional goals (e.g., rural care, research, primary care, urban underserved).
- Life experiences, background, and context matter: “Where have you come from to get to where you are? What illustrates resilience, drive, passion, commitment?”
- Admissions officers care what the applicant **learned** and how they plan to **apply** those lessons.

## 3. How Medical Schools Describe Their Audit / Selection Process

### University of Arizona College of Medicine – Phoenix

- **Metrics:** Competitive profile (e.g., MCAT percentiles) while building diverse classes. Dimensions of diversity can include first-gen (parent education/occupation), socioeconomic status, disadvantaged/underserved, Pell Grant, geographic (rural), URM.
- **Experiences:** Strong **distribution across five areas** in Work/Activities: (1) Clinical, (2) Research/Lab, (3) Leadership, (4) Community Service/Volunteer, (5) Extracurricular Activity/Hobby. Students who “balance it all” as undergraduates are seen as more likely to do so in medical school.
- **Attributes:** Emphasis on culture fit: e.g., “Servant Leaders” (personal humility + professional will), “Altruistic” (selfless givers).

### University of Pittsburgh School of Medicine

- **Interpersonal focus:** Desire and ability to interact with people; valuing person-to-person approach (small groups, patients from day one).
- **Academic:** Intellectual aptitude, excellent academic record.
- **Letters:** Pre-professional committee evaluations and letters of recommendation; special attention to letters from faculty with whom the applicant collaborated (scholarly and/or extracurricular).
- **Personal:** Resourceful, creative, leadership, potential to add to the profession; balance of academic achievement with “personal discovery” and “commitment to serving others.”
- **Competencies assessed:** Personal Attributes (service orientation, integrity, ethics, resilience, capacity for improvement); Communication (social/interpersonal, cultural competence); Healthcare Experience (insight into medicine, reliability, dependability); Leadership and Teamwork (service orientation, creativity); Research Experience; Academic performance.

### University of Nebraska Medical Center (UNMC)

- **Total assessment:** Motivation, interests, character, demonstrated intellectual ability, academic record (including trends), personal interview, MCAT, “general fitness and promise for a career in medicine.”
- **Academic:** Evaluated in context of course level and load, co-curriculars, employment, other factors.
- **Attributes:** Via letters of reference and interview.
- **Mission:** Strong desire to work in underserved communities and improve Nebraska’s health inequities.

### Cooper Medical School of Rowan University (CMSRU)

- **Holistic review:** Balanced consideration of experiences, attributes, and academic metrics; how the individual might contribute as a student and physician.
- **Pre-screen:** Ensures applicant is highly likely to succeed in the curriculum.
- **Mission fit:** Thriving in an **urban** environment; commitment to serving patients in communities with **limited access to healthcare**.
- **Interpersonal:** Well-developed interpersonal skills; working effectively with team and connecting with patients.
- **Service:** “Service to something larger than themselves”; service learning = real engagement with the community.
- **Academic:** Readiness demonstrated through coursework, MCAT, research, scholarship; “Are you ready now?” Academic paths vary; grades and test scores are important but do not measure all skills needed for success.

### Oakland University William Beaumont School of Medicine (OUWB)

- **Holistic review:** Full application file examined for fit with OUWB’s learning environment and the medical profession. Follows AAMC EAM (Experiences, Attributes, Metrics).
- **Grades and test scores:** Important predictors of academic performance, but do not capture all skills needed for a successful student or physician.
- **Desired criteria:** Intellectual curiosity and commitment to lifelong learning; excellence in an activity (sports, research, or other) at local, regional, or national level; ability to perform well academically; teamwork and leadership; service to others and community; **meaningful medical activities** (volunteer work, shadowing, etc.).
- **COVID-era note:** Longitudinal commitment to serving community, experiences outside comfort zone, commitment to the profession; virtual experiences considered; applicants should be able to speak meaningfully about experiences.

### Aggregated themes from other schools (Harvard, Stanford, UCSF, Ohio State, Temple, UCI, etc.)

- **No preference** for science majors over other majors, or for Ivy League / prestigious undergraduate institutions.
- **Academic:** GPA, MCAT, course rigor, trend (improvement or consistency). Prerequisites and recommended coursework vary by school.
- **Experiences:** Extracurriculars, leadership, clinical exposure (shadowing, volunteering, employment), research (biomedical or other), community service, work/summer experiences.
- **Attributes:** Integrity, emotional maturity, character, compassion, motivation, service orientation, ethical standards, adaptability, resilience, communication, collaboration, cultural competence.
- **Letters:** From faculty and others who know the applicant well; committee letters when available.
- **Interview:** Used to assess motivation, understanding of the profession and ethics, problem-solving, interpersonal skills, “the real you.”
- **Diversity:** Broad interests and talents; diversity of the student body enhances education; commitment to URM and mission-aligned populations.

## 4. How to Score a Pre-Health Resume (What to Count and How)

Use this section to assign **Smart**, **Grit**, and **Build** in a way that aligns with industry standards.

### Academic / Smart

- **GPA and BCPM:** Use only if stated on the resume. Weight science GPA (BCPM) when present for Pre-Health (e.g., 1.5× in Meridian’s rule set). Do not invent a GPA.
- **Major and rigor:** Consider major only as stated; do not assume “prestige” of institution. Focus on evidence of quantitative reasoning, scientific inquiry, living systems, human behavior (coursework, research, clinical context).
- **Honors and recognition:** Dean’s list, scholarships, honors programs, cum laude, etc., as evidence of commitment to learning and academic performance.
- **Research:** Publication, lab, bench work, sequencing, wet lab, PI role, etc., as evidence of scientific inquiry and living systems.

### Grit (Experiences and Attributes)

- **Quantifiable impact:** Numbers, percentages, dollar impact, people served, hours, outcomes. Evidence of reliability and impact.
- **Leadership density:** Titles and roles (president, founder, director, chair, lead, VP, manager, captain, etc.) as evidence of teamwork, leadership, service orientation.
- **Work/experience entries:** Sustained roles (use date ranges when present). Evidence of dependability and “balancing it all.”
- **International/global:** Study abroad, F-1/OPT/CPT, international student, exchange—can signal cultural awareness and adaptability (e.g., Global Grit +10% where applicable).

### Build (Pre-Health–Specific)

- **Clinical and patient-facing:** Clinical, shadowing, EMT, patient care, hospital, scribe, medical assistant, volunteer in clinical setting, surgery, direct patient care. Weight longitudinal (multi-year) involvement.
- **Research:** Has research (as above). Research longevity (e.g., 2+ years in same lab) can warrant a bonus in some rubrics.
- **Service and community:** Community service, volunteer work, service learning, underserved populations. Evidence of service orientation and cultural humility.
- **Leadership in health/service context:** Leadership in pre-health orgs, mentoring, tutoring, community roles.
- **Elite / “ready now”:** Schools often note GPA floors (e.g., 3.8+) for “elite” or highly competitive Pre-Health applicants. Use only if your scoring system defines such a floor and the resume states GPA.

## 5. How Students Can Improve Their Resume (Actionable Advice for Recommendations)

When giving **recommendations**, base them on gaps between this rubric and what appears on the resume. Use industry language (competencies, E-A-M, holistic review).

- **Missing clinical evidence:** “Add clinical or shadowing hours; include a clear date range (e.g., Month YYYY – Present) so committees can see longitudinal commitment.”
- **No research:** “Consider research or lab experience; even one sustained project demonstrates scientific inquiry and living systems.”
- **Weak distribution across experiences:** “Medical schools look for balance across clinical, research/lab, leadership, community service, and extracurriculars. Add or clarify at least one activity in [missing area].”
- **No quantifiable impact:** “Add numbers where possible: hours per week, people served, funds raised, percentage improvement, team size. This strengthens evidence of impact and reliability.”
- **Dates missing or vague:** “Use ‘Month YYYY’ for every role and activity so reviewers can assess duration and consistency.”
- **Leadership not visible:** “Name specific roles (e.g., President, Director, Lead) and outcomes so service orientation and leadership are clear.”
- **BCPM not listed:** “If you have a science GPA (BCPM), add it; many schools weight it heavily for Pre-Health.”
- **Pre-PT / Pre-OT intent not stated:** If the resume shows Physical Therapy or Occupational Therapy (e.g., concentration, clinic, observation hours) but does not explicitly say "Pre-PT," "Pre-OT," or "Pre-Health," recommend: "Explicitly state your pre-health track in Education or Objective (e.g. 'Pre-PT' or 'Pre-Physical Therapy') so reviewers and systems like Meridian can recognize your intent and surface track-specific scoring and recommendations."
- **Generic descriptions:** “Replace generic bullets with specific outcomes and competencies (e.g., teamwork, cultural humility, resilience) demonstrated in that role.”
