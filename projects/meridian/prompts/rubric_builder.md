# Builder (Recruiter / Employer) Rubric — Industry Standards

**Purpose:** This rubric is derived from published research and surveys of hiring managers, recruiters, and HR professionals (e.g., SHRM, ResumeTemplates/Jobseeker, Jobscan, eye-tracking and time-on-resume studies). Use it to score Builder-track resumes on the same factors that employers use when screening candidates. **Do not invent criteria; only apply what is stated here and what is evidenced in the resume.**

**Sources:** SHRM (Society for Human Resource Management) hiring and over/underqualified research; ResumeTemplates.com and Jobseeker hiring-manager surveys (2025–2026); Jobscan and InterviewPal resume-review data; Resumly/Indeed on quantifiable achievements; ATS and recruiter attention studies.

---

## 1. What Recruiters and Hiring Managers Prioritize (Top-Level Criteria)

### Most-Valued Traits (Survey-Based)

- **Communication** — Most important. Clear, concise, confident communication: in the resume (organization, clarity), in follow-ups, in interviews. Weak or unclear communication is a top rejection reason.
- **Professionalism** — Punctuality, preparedness, respectful tone, attention to detail. Typos, inconsistencies, and unprofessional tone lead to immediate rejection.
- **Adaptability** — Handling change, learning new tools, discussing examples of shifting priorities. Valued in fast-changing roles and teams.

### What Gets Screened First

- **Relevant job experience** — Single most important factor in initial screening (SHRM and recruiter research). Recruiters look for “fit”: enough experience to succeed without being overqualified for the role.
- **Work experience** — Most recruiters (e.g., ~82% in recent surveys) examine work experience first.
- **Quantifiable achievements** — Very high value (e.g., ~98% of hiring managers value them); a large share look at them in the first pass. Resumes with quantified achievements receive meaningfully more interview callbacks (e.g., ~40% in some studies).
- **Key skills** — Heavily prioritized; also central for ATS (applicant tracking systems) and keyword match.
- **Education** — Checked for match to role requirements.

## 2. Resume Sections and How They Are Evaluated

### Priority Order (What Recruiters Focus On)

1. **Contact information** — Must be present and correct.
2. **Professional summary / headline** — Often treated as a 30-second pitch; receives a large share of attention (e.g., ~24% in attention studies).
3. **Work experience** — Highest attention share (e.g., ~38%). Use **bullet points** (strongly preferred in surveys), **reverse-chronological** order, and **quantified achievements**.
4. **Skills and core competencies** — Critical for ATS and human review (e.g., ~18% of attention). Should align with job description and role.
5. **Education and certifications** — Checked for relevance and level.

### Format and Structure

- **Formatting matters:** A large majority of recruiters (e.g., ~96–98%) say formatting influences decisions. Clean, consistent layout, clear hierarchy, ample white space.
- **Bullet points** — Strongly preferred (e.g., ~80% in surveys); avoid dense paragraphs.
- **Length and scannability:** Many recruiters do not read past the first third of page one; single-column layouts and clear metrics can increase reading time and engagement.
- **Dates, company names, job titles** — Receive more attention than long job descriptions; ensure they are clear and consistent.

## 3. Quantifiable Achievements and Impact (Critical for Builder)

Employers value **concrete proof of impact**. Numbers make claims verifiable and differentiate candidates.

### Why Metrics Matter

- **Credibility:** “Increased customer satisfaction from 72% to 94%” is more credible than “improved customer satisfaction.”
- **Interview callbacks:** Resumes with quantified achievements perform significantly better in screening.
- **ATS:** Systems favor specific metrics (e.g., “increased sales by 27%”) over vague statements.
- **Memorability:** Specific figures stand out in recruiter review.

### Types of Metrics to Recognize and Reward

- **Percentages** — Growth, efficiency gains, improvement in KPIs.
- **Dollar amounts** — Revenue, savings, budget size, cost reduction.
- **Volume / frequency** — Throughput, workload, scale of projects or users.
- **Time** — Time saved, project duration, deadlines met.
- **Team / scale** — Team size led, number of stakeholders, scope of responsibility.
- **Rankings / comparisons** — Standing vs. peers, awards, benchmarks.

**Formula to look for:** Action verb + what was done + quantified result. Even approximate numbers are better than no metrics.

---

## 4. Experience “Fit” (SHRM and Recruiter Research)

- **Sweet spot:** Recruiters seek candidates with **enough** experience to succeed but not so much that they are clearly overqualified (retention and fit concerns).
- **Overqualified signals:** Very high salary expectations, current title well above the open role. Often flagged for short tenure or culture-fit risk.
- **Underqualified signals:** Experience below minimum requirement, insufficient education. Often flagged for training burden or performance risk.
- **Relevant experience** is weighed most heavily; “relevant” means aligned with the role and industry.

## 5. Fastest Ways to Get Rejected (What to Penalize or Note as Gaps)

When something is missing or weak, it can justify a lower score or a recommendation. Do not invent; only note what is present or absent on the resume.

- **Weak communication** — Unclear, casual, or unprofessional tone; poor organization.
- **Poor attention to detail** — Typos, inconsistent dates or titles, missing information.
- **Weak problem-solving evidence** — No examples of analysis, optimization, or impact.
- **Dense text, bad formatting** — Long blocks of text, inconsistent fonts or structure, no bullets.
- **Unclear career progression** — Gaps unexplained, titles or roles that don’t build a coherent story.
- **No quantifiable impact** — Only duties listed, no outcomes or metrics.
- **Skills not visible** — Missing or generic skills section; no alignment with common tools (e.g., tech stack, software, methodologies).

## 6. How to Score a Builder Resume (What to Count and How)

### Smart (Academic / Analytical)

- **GPA and major:** Use only if stated. Weight quantitative/technical majors where your system defines it (e.g., CS, Data Science, Math). Do not invent GPA.
- **Education level and certifications:** Match to role level; relevant certs (e.g., cloud, security, data) as evidence of commitment to learning.
- **Honors and recognition:** Scholarships, dean’s list, awards as evidence of performance and drive.

### Grit (Impact, Leadership, Experience Density)

- **Quantifiable impact count:** Count explicit metrics (%, $, numbers) in bullets. Each clear metric is evidence of impact; weight heavily.
- **Leadership density:** Titles and roles (lead, manager, director, founder, president, VP, etc.) as evidence of responsibility and collaboration.
- **Work/experience entries:** Number of distinct roles or projects with clear dates. Sustained tenure and progression support reliability and adaptability.

### Build (Track-Specific — Tech / Projects / Execution)

- **Tech stack and tools:** Keywords such as Python, SQL, JavaScript, AWS, Docker, Excel, Tableau, React, Git, machine learning, Pandas, R, Java, TypeScript. Weight depth (multiple tools, projects using them).
- **Projects and delivery:** Evidence of building, developing, creating, deploying (e.g., “Built X,” “Developed Y,” “Deployed Z”). Count as project/execution signals.
- **Commit velocity / research density:** If evidenced (e.g., GitHub, contributions, research semesters), can warrant a bonus in rubrics that define it.
- **Format and clarity:** Clean structure, bullets, dates, and metrics support “professionalism” and “communication” as defined by employers.

## 7. How Candidates Can Improve Their Resume (Actionable Recommendations)

Base recommendations on gaps between this rubric and the resume. Use industry language (recruiter priorities, ATS, quantifiable impact).

- **No or few numbers:** “Add quantifiable impact: percentages, dollar impact, team size, time saved, or scale (e.g., users, transactions). Resumes with metrics get significantly more callbacks.”
- **Duties only:** “Replace duty-only bullets with outcome-focused bullets: Action verb + what you did + quantified result.”
- **Missing dates or vague roles:** “Use ‘Month YYYY’ for every role so recruiters can assess duration and progression.”
- **Weak or missing skills section:** “Add a clear skills section aligned with the target role (tools, languages, methods). This helps both ATS and human reviewers.”
- **Dense or inconsistent formatting:** “Use bullet points, consistent headings, and white space. Most recruiters say formatting influences their decision.”
- **No project or build evidence:** “Add 1–2 concrete projects or deployments: what you built, with which tools, and what the outcome was.”
- **Leadership not visible:** “Spell out leadership roles and scope (e.g., ‘Led team of X,’ ‘Managed Y’).”
- **Generic summary:** “Turn the summary into a short, specific pitch: key skills, domain, and one concrete outcome.”
