How to Get an Internship With No Experience: A Freshman & Sophomore Guide

Every student applying to internships for the first time faces the same problem: the listings say they want experience, and you don't have any. This feels like an impossible loop. It isn't. The students who land their first internship as freshmen or sophomores aren't doing something magical. They're doing a few specific things that most of their peers aren't.

First: Understand What "No Experience" Actually Means

When a student says they have no experience, they usually mean they haven't had a formal internship before. But experience in the context of an internship application is broader than that. It includes part-time jobs, campus roles, class projects, volunteer work, research assistant positions, and anything you've built or contributed to outside of class.

Almost every student has more of this than they think. The problem isn't usually an absence of experience. It's an inability to recognize what counts and how to describe it. If you've held any campus job, led any club role, completed any class project with a deliverable, or done anything with real scope and responsibility, you have something to work with.

What to Build Before You Apply

If you're genuinely early — first semester, nothing on your resume yet — the most productive thing you can do is spend four to six weeks building credentials before you start applying. This is counterintuitive. Most students start applying immediately and get filtered out. The ones who build first and apply second have much better outcomes.

Get a campus role with real responsibility

RA, peer tutor, research assistant, campus ambassador, undergraduate lab position — these are available to most students and accessible without prior experience. A campus role that involves managing something, leading someone, or producing something tangible is meaningful on a resume. It signals maturity and work ethic. Apply to campus jobs in your first week if you want them on your resume by recruiting season.

Join a club with an officer path

Join clubs in September of your first year, not October. Most clubs run elections in September. If you show up in October, you've already missed the first recruitment and officer cycle. Pick one club you actually care about and participate actively. A genuine officer role by sophomore year is worth more on a resume than five clubs where you're just a member.

Do a project in your intended field

A self-directed project takes four to six weeks and costs nothing. For a CS student, it's a small app or script. For a finance student, it's a stock analysis writeup. For a marketing student, it's a case study on a brand campaign. For a pre-med student, it's a literature review or a volunteer research position. The project doesn't have to be impressive in scope. It needs to be specific, real, and something you can describe in concrete terms.

The project doesn't have to be big Students consistently underestimate what qualifies as a project. A Python script that does one useful thing. A 10-page analysis of a company's financials. A small event you organized and ran. A dataset you cleaned and visualized. If you built it, finished it, and can describe what it does in two sentences, it counts.

Where to Actually Look for Your First Internship

Freshman and sophomore students often make the mistake of applying to the same places juniors apply. JP Morgan, Google, McKinsey — these programs are highly competitive, often explicitly require upperclassmen, and receive thousands of applications. That doesn't mean you shouldn't apply. It means those can't be your only targets.

Look for freshman/sophomore-specific programs

Many large companies run dedicated programs for underclassmen precisely because they want to build a pipeline early. Google STEP, Microsoft Explore, Bank of America Freshman Summer Analyst, JPMorgan Winning Women, Morgan Stanley Early Insights — these programs are designed for freshmen and sophomores and are far less competitive than standard junior recruiting. Research what exists in your industry. Most of these have October and November application deadlines.

Target small companies and startups

A 30-person startup that needs someone to help with marketing, operations, data, or engineering doesn't have the luxury of waiting for a junior with two prior internships. They need someone smart and available now. Small companies are often where you get your best first experience anyway — broader responsibilities, more direct mentorship, real ownership of projects.

Local and regional businesses

Your city has hundreds of businesses that could benefit from an intern and don't receive a single application because students don't think to look locally. A cold email to a local financial advisory firm, a small tech company, a marketing agency, or a healthcare practice can turn into an internship that a student who only applied to name-brand companies never found. The competition is essentially zero.

Research labs and faculty

If you're in any science, data, or social science field, emailing professors directly is one of the highest-return activities you can do as a freshman. Most professors welcome undergraduates in their labs, and the experience of a research assistant position — even unpaid — opens doors to future paid positions, graduate school recommendations, and strong resume content.

How to Write a Resume When You Have Almost Nothing

Lead with Education. As a freshman or sophomore, your academic record is still your strongest signal. Include your GPA if it's above 3.3, your relevant coursework, any academic awards, and your expected graduation date.

In the Experience section, include everything with real responsibility: part-time jobs, campus jobs, babysitting done consistently and at scale (if it actually was), tutoring, coaching. Describe what you did, not just the title. "Tutored 4 high school students in AP Calculus over 8 months, all of whom passed the exam" is a real resume bullet.

Lean hard on Projects. A class project that produced a tangible deliverable belongs in a Projects section with a description of what you built and what tools you used. Three solid project bullets are worth more than an empty Experience section.

Keep it to one page, every time. No padding. No objective statement. Contact information, Education, Experience or Projects, Skills, Activities. That's it.

How to Apply Without Getting Immediately Filtered Out

The biggest mistake freshmen make: applying with a resume that doesn't pass automated screening. ATS systems filter resumes before a human ever sees them. A resume with inconsistent formatting, non-standard section names, graphics, tables, columns in two-column layouts, or unusual fonts can fail to parse correctly and get rejected for formatting reasons that have nothing to do with your qualifications.

Use a clean, single-column format. Standard section names: Education, Experience, Skills, Projects, Activities. PDF output. No headers in the document header field, no footers, no text boxes. When an ATS parses your resume, it should find exactly what you wrote.

Tailor each application by matching your language to the job description. If the company's posting says "data analysis" and your project involved data analysis, use those words. You're not gaming the system. You're communicating in the terms the hiring team used when they defined the role.

The Honest Expectation

Your first internship application season will likely involve more rejections than offers. That is true for everyone, including the students who end up at the best companies. The difference between students who land something and students who don't is usually not talent. It's preparation — starting earlier, building credentials before applying, targeting the right companies, and having a resume that actually communicates what they've done.
